Cambridge Building Permits: What 311 Recent Filings Tell Contractors

Cambridge is a mechanical-systems town right now

We're tracking 311 recent building permits in Cambridge, MA, and the trade mix tells a specific story. HVAC leads the pack with 52 permits — more than any other trade — followed by general building work at 45 and electrical at 39. Add gas at 26 and you have a city where the biggest share of permitted work is happening inside the walls and mechanical rooms, not on new foundations.

That pattern fits Cambridge's housing stock. Streets showing up in recent filings — Putnam Ave, Trowbridge St, Avon St, Sacramento St, Franklin St — are lined with older triple-deckers and pre-war homes whose heating, wiring, and gas systems are due for replacement. When an owner on Huron Ave or Banks St pulls an HVAC or electrical permit, it's usually a heat pump conversion, a panel upgrade, or a system swap in a building that's decades overdue for one. For HVAC and electrical contractors, Cambridge is one of the deepest replacement markets in Greater Boston.

Solar and demolition round out the picture

Two more signals stand out. Solar accounts for 24 permits — nearly as many as gas — which puts Cambridge among the more active electrification markets we track. Solar installs frequently trigger companion electrical work, so those 24 filings are really leads for two trades. And 21 demolition permits plus 22 roofing permits point to teardown-and-rebuild activity and envelope work, with recent filings ranging from residential side streets like Marney St and Lambert St out to Fawcett St and Binney St near the commercial corridors.

How a fresh permit becomes a paying job

A permit filing is the single strongest buying signal in construction. The owner has committed money, scoped work, and a timeline — and in most cases they still need more trades than the one they filed for. Here's how contractors work Cambridge permit data:

  • Sell the adjacent trade. A demolition permit on Putnam Ave means a rebuild is coming — framing, roofing, electrical, and HVAC bids all have a window before the GC locks in subs.
  • Reach owners while the project is live. An owner who just filed for a gas-to-heat-pump conversion on Trowbridge St is actively comparing installers. A call that week beats a mailer next quarter.
  • Follow the clusters. When one block shows multiple filings, neighbors are watching the work. A crew already on Sacramento St can door-knock the street at near-zero acquisition cost.
