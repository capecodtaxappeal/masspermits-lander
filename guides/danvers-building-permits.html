The Danvers trade mix: general building leads, backed by heavy mechanical work

Of the 243 tracked filings, the breakdown looks like this:

  • HVAC — 42 permits
  • Gas — 23 permits
  • Plumbing — 22 permits
  • Electrical — 20 permits
  • Fire/Sprinkler — 19 permits

Two things stand out. First, general building permits alone (82) roughly equal HVAC and gas combined (65) — Danvers homeowners are doing structural and renovation work, not just swapping equipment. Second, the mechanical trades stack up: HVAC, gas and plumbing together account for 87 filings, more than any single category. Every one of those jobs typically pulls follow-on work — a new heating system often means gas piping, electrical upgrades and sometimes carpentry to close it all back up.

The 19 pool/spa permits are a distinctly Danvers signal. Pool installs generate some of the richest downstream leads in residential construction: fencing, electrical bonding, gas for heaters, patios and landscaping all follow the shell going in the ground.

How a fresh Danvers permit becomes your next job

A building permit is the earliest reliable public signal that a project is real. The owner has plans, a budget and usually a schedule. Here is why reaching them right after they file wins work:

  • The project is funded but not finished. Most permits cover one scope. The homeowner replacing a furnace still needs the electrician, the plumber, the drywaller. If you are the adjacent trade, the permit is your introduction.
  • You reach them before they start searching. A knock, letter or call that says "I saw you pulled a permit on High St" lands as informed, not cold. You are competing against nobody, because they have not posted the next scope anywhere yet.
  • Clusters compound. When several permits land on the same street — as they have recently on Paulette Dr and Newbury St — one visible job site becomes a neighborhood marketing asset.
