Edgartown Building Permits: What 145 Recent Filings Say About Where the Work Is

Edgartown is one of the most active permit markets on Martha's Vineyard, and the filings tell a specific story. Across 145 recent permits tracked by MassPermits, building permits lead the pack at 34 — the single largest trade category in town. But what's striking is how evenly the mechanical trades follow: gas and plumbing are tied at 24 permits each, with electrical and solar tied right behind at 23 each, and pool/spa work rounding out the top six at 18.

That's not a market dominated by one trade. It's a market where whole-house projects are pulling every trade in behind them — and where a contractor watching the permit feed can see the follow-on work coming before the phone ever rings.

What the Edgartown trade mix means for contractors

When building permits outnumber every individual mechanical trade, but gas, plumbing, and electrical each run close behind, you're looking at renovation and new-construction activity that cascades. A building permit filed on Oyster Pond Rd or Slough Cove Rd today is very often a gas, plumbing, and electrical job next month.

Two other signals stand out in the Edgartown data:

  • Solar is tied with electrical at 23 permits. On a per-capita basis for a town this size, that is a serious solar market — island electricity costs and large south-facing roofs on newer builds are driving real installation volume.
  • Pool/spa comes in at 18 permits — the smallest of the top six trades, but still substantial. Pool projects in Edgartown tend to travel with the high-end residential work clustered in areas like Mattakesett Way and North Neck Rd.

Where the work is happening

Recent filings span the range of Edgartown's neighborhoods: repeat activity on Morse St near the village center, waterfront-adjacent projects on Oyster Pond Rd and Slough Cove Rd, Chappaquiddick work out on North Neck Rd, and filings on Holly Bear Ln, Mariners Way, Pilgrim Rd, and Bold Meadow Rd. Multiple permits on the same street — Morse St and Holly Bear Ln both appear repeatedly in the recent feed — usually means either a large multi-phase project or a block where neighbors are renovating in waves. Either way, that's a street worth knowing about.

How a fresh permit becomes a job

A permit filing is the earliest public signal that a property owner is spending money on their home. Here's why that timing matters:

  • The owner is in active project mode. They've hired at least one contractor, but the subs and follow-on trades often aren't locked in yet. A building permit rarely means the solar installer, pool builder, or landscaper is already chosen.
  • You reach them before your competitors do. Most contractors find out about a project when the owner calls three of them for quotes. The contractor who reaches out first — informed, specific, referencing the actual work being done — skips that lineup entirely.
  • Island logistics reward early contact. On the Vineyard, scheduling around the boat, material deliveries, and seasonal crunch means owners value contractors who show up early and organized. A timely, relevant outreach reads as professionalism, not cold-calling.

Driving around looking for dumpsters works, but it means the job is already staffed. For contractors looking for jobs in Edgartown MA, the math is simple: 145 recent filings, six trades all running double-digit volume, and every one of those filings is a property owner actively writing checks. The only question is who reaches them first.
