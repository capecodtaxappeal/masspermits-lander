Hanover's permit activity, by the numbers

That distribution is unusually flat outside the HVAC spike. In many South Shore towns, one or two trades dominate. In Hanover, six of the seven tracked trades sit within a few permits of each other, which means steady, diversified work — not a single boom category that could dry up next quarter.

What the trade mix means for contractors

The HVAC lead — 36 permits, more than one in five filings — points to a wave of system replacements and upgrades in Hanover's housing stock. When homeowners on streets like Old Town Way, Juniper Lane, and Larchmont Lane pull HVAC permits, plumbing, gas, and electrical work often follow: the gas count (17) and electrical count (19) in the same window suggest exactly that kind of paired mechanical work.

The septic number deserves attention too. Twenty septic permits is a meaningful share for a town Hanover's size, and it reflects a reality of the area: large portions of town are not on municipal sewer. Septic filings on roads like Berry St, Bailey Road, and Olde Forge Road are high-ticket jobs, and each one usually signals a homeowner already spending — a prime moment for excavation, landscaping, and plumbing contractors to introduce themselves.

The fire/sprinkler count (18) alongside 23 building permits hints at renovation and addition work substantial enough to trigger life-safety requirements, concentrated along corridors like Washington St, Main St, and King St.

How a fresh permit becomes a job

A permit filing is the single strongest buying signal in residential construction. Here's why it converts:

  • The owner has already decided to spend. Nobody files a Hanover building permit for fun. The project is funded and moving.
  • Adjacent work is unassigned. A homeowner who filed a building permit on Union St or Water St often hasn't yet hired the electrician, plumber, or HVAC contractor the project will need.
  • Timing is everything. The window between filing and contractor selection is short. Reaching an owner the week they file beats any amount of general advertising, because you're the only contractor in the conversation.

That's the core of how Hanover construction leads work: instead of bidding against five companies on a marketplace, you contact one homeowner who just demonstrated intent, before your competitors know the project exists.
