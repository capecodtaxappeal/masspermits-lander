What Harwich's trade mix says about the work

Of the 213 recent filings, Building permits lead by a wide margin at 73 — more than the next two categories combined. That's the signature of a renovation-heavy town: additions, structural work, and whole-home projects on Cape housing stock, from village streets like Pleasant St and Oak St to waterside addresses along Strandway and Long Pond Dr.

Behind building, the mechanical trades run in a tight cluster:

  • Plumbing — 25 permits, the busiest of the sub-trades
  • Gas — 24 and HVAC — 23, nearly tied
  • Electrical — 22 and Septic — 22, dead even
  • Demolition — 13, the smallest slice tracked

Two things stand out. First, septic pulling even with electrical is unusual — it reflects Harwich's reliance on private systems, and it means excavation and septic contractors have a real, permit-visible market here. Second, 13 demolition permits is a leading indicator: demo filings on streets like Monomoy Rd or Beach Hill Rd today usually mean rebuild, foundation, framing, and finish work on the same parcels in the months ahead.

How a fresh permit becomes a job

Every Harwich building permit is a public record with an exact address, an owner, a project type, and — often — a contractor of record. For a tradesperson, that's a lead with the guesswork removed:

  • A building permit on Bentley Rd or Caldwell Ln means an owner who is actively spending on that house right now. The plumbing, electrical, HVAC, and gas phases of that project may not be contracted yet.
  • A demolition permit is the earliest possible signal of a major rebuild — reach that owner before the project is fully bid and you're competing with nobody.
  • A septic permit flags landscaping, hardscape repair, and irrigation work that almost always follows the excavation.

The contractor who calls the week the permit is filed is talking to a motivated owner mid-project. The contractor who waits for referrals is talking to whoever's left. That timing edge is the whole case for permit-based Harwich construction leads: the filing itself proves budget, intent, and an active jobsite.
