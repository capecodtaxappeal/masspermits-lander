Quincy Building Permits: What 160 Recent Filings Say About Where the Work Is

Quincy is one of the busiest permit markets on the South Shore, and the filings tell a clear story. Of the 160 recent Quincy building permits we're tracking, mechanical work dominates: HVAC leads with 45 permits and gas is right behind at 38. Together those two trades account for more than half of all recent activity in the city. That is what you'd expect from Quincy's housing stock — dense neighborhoods of older two- and three-family homes from Wollaston to Quincy Point, where aging heating systems are being swapped for high-efficiency units and heat pumps, and every conversion drags a gas permit along with it.

The Quincy trade mix, by the numbers

  • HVAC — 45 permits. The single largest trade in the city right now.
  • Gas — 38 permits. Piping, meter work, and appliance hookups riding alongside the HVAC wave.
  • Building — 21 permits. General construction, additions, and renovations.
  • Electrical — 19 permits and demolition — 19 permits, tied. That demolition number is unusually high for a city this size — teardowns and gut renovations are a real part of the Quincy market.
  • Plumbing — 18 permits. Steady baseline work.

Where the work is happening

Recent filings cluster on streets contractors will recognize: Newport Ave and Willard St along the commercial corridors, Beale St in Wollaston, Highland Ave and Sagamore Ave in the residential neighborhoods, and Seaport Dr and Victory Rd out at Marina Bay — where both streets show multiple recent permits. When the same street shows up more than once in a short window, that's a block in motion, and neighbors on a block in motion pull permits next.

How a fresh permit becomes your next job

A building permit is the single strongest buying signal in construction. The owner has already decided to spend, hired at least one trade, and passed the city's counter. Here's why that matters for Quincy construction leads:

  • One permit rarely travels alone. A demolition permit precedes a building permit. A building permit pulls electrical, plumbing, gas, and HVAC behind it. Quincy's 19 demolition filings are tomorrow's general-contracting jobs; its 21 building permits are next month's mechanical and finish work.
  • Timing beats everything. The owner who filed last week is actively hiring subs right now. The owner who filed last year already has a full roster. Reaching out while the project is live means you're bidding real scope, not leaving voicemails.
  • You're contacting a proven spender. Cold lists are full of maybes. A permit holder has already written checks. Your pitch isn't "are you doing a project?" — it's "I work this trade on your street."

Winning contractor jobs in Quincy, MA

If you're an HVAC or gas contractor, Quincy should be at the top of your route list — the volume is there and it's the deepest trade pool in the city. Electricians and plumbers should watch the building and demolition filings, because those 40 combined permits are upstream of their phase of work. And general contractors should treat the demolition list as a pipeline: in a built-out city like Quincy, a teardown is almost always the first line of a much bigger project.
