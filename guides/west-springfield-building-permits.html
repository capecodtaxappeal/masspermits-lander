How a fresh permit becomes your next job

Every permit is a public record that says three things: a specific property owner, at a specific West Springfield address, is spending money on their building right now. That is the strongest buying signal that exists in the trades.

The play is rarely to chase the permit that was just pulled — that job is usually taken. The value is in what surrounds it:

  • Adjacent trades. A gas permit on Lyman St often means an electrician is needed for the new equipment, or a plumber for the water heater that gets replaced at the same time. With 47 gas and 42 HVAC permits in the recent batch, electricians and plumbers in West Springfield have a long list of homes already mid-project.
  • Neighborhood clustering. Recent filings show runs of activity on single streets — Morton St alone shows a string of consecutive permits. When one house on a street replaces a furnace, the neighbors with the same-age equipment are next. Door hangers and mailers to the surrounding block convert because the timing is right.
  • Follow-on work. The 37 building permits are tomorrow's finish work: owners mid-renovation on Park Ave or Sawmill Rd will need painters, flooring, electrical, and plumbing before the job closes out.
