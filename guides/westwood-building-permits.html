Westwood Building Permits: Where the Work Is Right Now

Look at the 169 most recent building permits filed in Westwood and one thing jumps out: this is a town where general building work and plumbing are running neck and neck. Building permits lead the pack at 43 filings, with plumbing right behind at 42 — an almost even split that tells you Westwood homeowners aren't just patching roofs. They're doing the kind of interior renovations — kitchens, baths, additions — where a carpenter and a plumber end up on the same job site.

The trade mix: mechanicals dominate in aggregate

Here's the breakdown from the current permit feed:

  • Building — 43 permits. The single largest category. Structural work, renovations, and additions.
  • Plumbing — 42 permits. Practically tied with building — a strong signal of bath and kitchen remodels rather than exterior-only work.
  • HVAC — 25 permits. Heating and cooling swaps are steady business here.
  • Gas — 20 permits. Often paired with HVAC and plumbing jobs on the same address.
  • Electrical — 16 permits.
  • Pool/Spa — 12 permits. A dozen pool and spa filings is a notable niche for a town this size.
  • Fire/Sprinkler — 5 permits. The smallest slice, mostly tied to larger projects.

Add up HVAC, gas, and electrical and the mechanical trades combine for 61 permits — more than the building category alone. If you're a mechanical contractor wondering whether Westwood is worth your windshield time, the answer in this feed is yes.

Where the filings are landing

Recent permits cluster on residential streets like Churchill Rd (which shows up more than once in the current feed), Stanford Dr, Tamarack Rd, Gay St, Pond St, and Reynolds Farm Way, alongside commercial-corridor addresses on Providence Hwy and Canton St. That mix matters: the neighborhood streets are owner-occupied renovation work, while the Providence Hwy corridor filings tend to be commercial fit-outs — two very different sales conversations, both visible in the same permit stream.

How a fresh permit becomes your next job

A building permit is the earliest public signal that money is being spent at a specific address. The moment a Westwood homeowner pulls a permit, you know three things: the project is real, it's funded, and it's happening now. What most contractors miss is that one permit usually spawns others. A building permit on Pheasant Hill St or Wells Ave often means plumbing, electrical, and HVAC scopes are about to be bid — sometimes before the owner has even chosen those subs.

That's the window. Reaching an owner in the week after they file beats any amount of postcard blasting to a cold list, because you're contacting someone who is provably mid-project. If you do decks, the homeowner who just permitted an addition may want one next. If you do HVAC, the owner who just filed a plumbing permit for a bath remodel may be a furnace customer this fall. Permit data turns "who might need work someday" into "who is spending on their house this month."
